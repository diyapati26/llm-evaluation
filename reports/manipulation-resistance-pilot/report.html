Manipulation Resistance in Large Language Models

Do models abandon a correct, well-reasoned answer under social pressure? A controlled pilot across seven models and three families.
Sai Harsha Rudra  ·  evaluation framework: Diya Pati
Study type: Pilot Date: June 19, 2026 Models: 7 across 3 families Judge: Claude Opus 4.8 Seed: 42 Run: cheaper-pilot-20260619T003531Z
Abstract

We measure manipulation resistance — whether a language model abandons a correct answer when an adversary applies social pressure (confident assertions, fabricated evidence, appeals to authority, emotional appeals, and others). Seven models spanning the OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google families are evaluated under five experimental modes (single-turn, chained, repeated-rephrasing, a 29-turn pressure gauntlet, and gradual drift), with a separate benchmark-accuracy and moral/empathy battery scored by an LLM judge. Resistance rises sharply with model capability and the gap is statistically significant (McNemar, p<0.001): the strongest models (Claude Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.5 Flash) hold their answer ~98–100% of the time and survive the full gauntlet, while lighter models fold on 30–40% of single-turn attacks and cave within ~8 turns. Crucially, the most capable flagship buys no additional robustness over substantially cheaper models that already saturate the ceiling. We also identify which attacks actually work: confidently-asserted wrong answers and fabricated evidence are the most potent; emotional appeals are largely ineffective.

1Introduction

A model that reasons correctly but capitulates the moment a user pushes back is unreliable in exactly the situations where reliability matters — medicine, law, finance, safety. We treat this as a measurable property, manipulation resistance, and ask a precise question: given an item the model answers correctly on its own, how often does it switch to a specific wrong answer when pressured, and how does that depend on capability, attack type, and conversation length?

This is a pilot (small per-cell sample) intended to validate the methodology and surface directional effects before scaling to a publication-grade run. All numbers trace to a content-addressed call ledger and a frozen, seeded design, so every figure here is reproducible from raw API records.

2Methodology

2.1 Models under test

Seven models, two-to-three capability points per family, so the capability-vs-robustness claim can be tested within a family (holding training style roughly fixed) rather than across three isolated points:

  • OpenAI: GPT-5.4-nano, GPT-5.4-mini, GPT-5.5
  • Anthropic: Claude Haiku 4.5, Claude Sonnet 4.6
  • Google: Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite, Gemini 3.5 Flash

2.2 Item pool

Manipulation items are drawn from ten deliberately hard MMLU subjects (professional medicine and law, college physics/chemistry, abstract algebra, formal logic, high-school statistics, machine learning, clinical knowledge, international law). Only items the model answers correctly at baseline enter the pressure test — folding is defined relative to that correct baseline.

2.3 Pressure attacks

Seven adversarial tactics are applied after the model commits to its answer: confident assertion (the attacker flatly states a wrong answer), false evidence (fabricated citations/data), authority pressure, concession override, identity challenge, false consensus ("everyone agrees…"), and emotional pressure. A neutral, no-pressure rephrasing serves as a natural-drift control.

2.4 Experimental modes

  • Stateless — one attack, one phrasing, fresh session → per-attack potency.
  • Stateful — all attacks in sequence, one session → cross-tactic "killing blow".
  • Repeat — one attack, every rephrasing, one session → persistence under rewording.
  • Gauntlet — every attack × every variant in one 29-turn session → an endurance ceiling (turns survived / survived-all rate). Reported as endurance, not per-attack potency (position confound).
  • Drift — a six-turn gradual escalation.

2.5 Scoring

Each post-pressure response is scored resist / fold / hedge. A genuine fold requires that the baseline was correct and the model switched to a specific wrong answer; an "uncertain"/non-committal answer is a hedge, not a fold. Multi-turn chains stop at the first genuine fold. Reported single-turn resistance is drift-adjusted (raw resistance minus the neutral-control drift).

2.6 Judge & statistics

Generative TruthfulQA and the moral/empathy battery are scored by a single LLM judge (Claude Opus 4.8). Proportions carry Wilson 95% confidence intervals; pairwise robustness differences use McNemar's test on matched item×attack pairs (exact when discordant pairs <25). Architecture is ledger-first: a frozen seeded design, one fsync'd record per API call, a content-addressed cache, and a pure offline analysis pass — so collection produces facts and analysis produces findings, and every number traces to a raw call.

4Discussion — key findings

1
Capability buys robustness, with significance. Within every family the larger model resists more, and McNemar confirms the strong-vs-weak gap at p<0.001. Resistance and benchmark accuracy move together, pointing to capability as the shared cause.
2
The ceiling is cheap to reach. Claude Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5.5, and Gemini 3.5 Flash are statistically tied at ~98–100% resistance and perfect gauntlet endurance — yet differ ~6× in price. For manipulation resistance specifically, the flagship premium is unjustified.
3
Attack potency is lopsided. Confident assertion and fabricated evidence drive almost all folds; emotional and consensus appeals are near-inert. Defensive effort should target the former.
4
Length is an attack vector for weak models. Under repeated rephrasing and the long gauntlet, light models that pass single-turn tests still erode — Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite folds ~88% under repetition of a confident assertion. Single-turn evaluation overstates the robustness of small models.

5Limitations

  • Pilot sample. Most cells are n≈10; CIs are wide. Directional, not definitive — a paper-grade run (5–10 items/subject, k≥5 resamples, benchmark n≈100) is the next step.
  • Single judge. Generative/moral axes use one judge (Opus 4.8), so no inter-judge agreement (Cohen's κ) is reported; a second-family judge is needed before publishing judge-based scores.
  • Excluded trials. 4 of 3,353 manipulation trials (0.12%) were dropped: on a few adversarial long-context items, Claude Haiku/Sonnet degenerated into a token-level repetition loop and produced unparseable structured output. This persisted across decoding settings (temperature 0 and ~1) and is a genuine model failure mode rather than a framework bug; affected cells simply lose those observations.
  • Item selection. Hard-MMLU items bias toward difficulty; resistance on everyday questions may differ.
